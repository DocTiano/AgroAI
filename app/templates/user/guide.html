Crop Disease Management Guide

This guide provides information about common crop diseases, their symptoms, and management strategies.

About This Guide

The AgroAI Disease Management Guide is designed to help farmers and gardeners identify and manage common crop diseases. Each disease entry includes:

  • Description: An overview of the disease and its impact
  • Symptoms: Key visual indicators to help with identification
  • Treatment: Recommended actions for infected plants
  • Prevention: Strategies to avoid future infections

Use the search box to find specific diseases or the dropdown menu to filter by plant type. For more detailed analysis, you can use our AI-powered disease detection tools in the Upload section.

Tomato Late Blight

Phytophthora infestans

Description

Late blight is a devastating disease of tomato and potato that caused the Irish potato famine in the 1840s. It can destroy a crop within days under favorable conditions.

Symptoms
  • Dark, water-soaked lesions on leaves, stems, and fruits
  • White, fuzzy growth on the underside of leaves
  • Rapid browning and wilting of foliage
  • Firm, dark, greasy-looking lesions on fruits
Treatment
  • Apply copper-based fungicides
  • Remove and destroy infected plants
  • Improve air circulation around plants
  • Destroy volunteer tomato and potato plants
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Avoid overhead irrigation
  • Space plants properly
  • Practice crop rotation
Apple Scab

Venturia inaequalis

Description

Apple scab is one of the most common diseases of apple trees, affecting both leaves and fruit. It is caused by a fungus and thrives in cool, wet spring weather.

Symptoms
  • Olive-green to brown spots on leaves
  • Infected leaves may curl and fall prematurely
  • Dark, scabby lesions on fruit
  • Scabby, deformed fruit
  • Cracking of severely infected fruit
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing captan or myclobutanil
  • Remove and destroy fallen leaves
  • Prune trees to improve air circulation
  • Thin fruit clusters to prevent disease spread
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Apply preventive fungicides before bud break
  • Rake and destroy fallen leaves
  • Maintain proper tree spacing
Corn Northern Leaf Blight

Exserohilum turcicum

Description

Northern leaf blight is a fungal disease that affects corn, reducing yield and quality. It is most severe during humid weather with moderate temperatures.

Symptoms
  • Long, elliptical, grayish-green to tan lesions on leaves
  • Lesions may coalesce, causing entire leaves to die
  • Dark spores may form on lesions during humid conditions
  • Premature death of leaves above the ear
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing azoxystrobin or propiconazole
  • Remove and destroy infected crop debris
  • Maintain balanced soil fertility
Prevention
  • Plant resistant hybrids
  • Rotate crops
  • Plow under crop debris
  • Apply preventive fungicides at early signs of disease
  • Avoid overhead irrigation
Potato Late Blight

Phytophthora infestans

Description

Late blight is a devastating disease of potato and tomato. It spreads rapidly in cool, wet conditions and can destroy entire fields within days if not controlled.

Symptoms
  • Water-soaked spots on leaves that quickly turn brown or black
  • White fuzzy growth on leaf undersides in humid conditions
  • Rapid wilting and death of foliage
  • Brown, firm rot in tubers
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing chlorothalonil or mancozeb
  • Remove and destroy infected plants
  • Harvest potatoes early if disease appears late in season
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Use certified disease-free seed potatoes
  • Ensure good air circulation
  • Practice crop rotation
  • Apply preventive fungicides during high-risk periods
Potato Early Blight

Alternaria solani

Description

Early blight is a common fungal disease that affects potatoes worldwide. It primarily affects the foliage but can also cause tuber rot in storage.

Symptoms
  • Dark brown to black lesions with concentric rings (target-like pattern)
  • Yellowing of tissue surrounding lesions
  • Lesions may coalesce, causing leaves to wither and die
  • Shallow, dark, sunken lesions on tubers
  • Premature defoliation and reduced yield
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing chlorothalonil or copper
  • Remove and destroy infected plant material
  • Maintain adequate soil fertility
  • Avoid overhead irrigation
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Use certified disease-free seed potatoes
  • Practice crop rotation (2-3 years)
  • Space plants properly for good air circulation
  • Apply preventive fungicides before symptoms appear
Potato Black Leg

Pectobacterium spp.

Description

Black leg is a bacterial disease that affects potato plants, causing stem rot and tuber decay. It is most severe in wet, cool conditions and poorly drained soils.

Symptoms
  • Black, slimy decay at the base of stems
  • Yellowing and wilting of lower leaves
  • Stunted growth and upward rolling of leaves
  • Soft, wet rot of tubers with a foul odor
  • Plants may collapse and die
Treatment
  • No effective chemical treatments once infected
  • Remove and destroy infected plants
  • Improve drainage in affected areas
  • Avoid harvesting in wet conditions
Prevention
  • Use certified disease-free seed potatoes
  • Avoid planting in poorly drained soils
  • Practice crop rotation (3-4 years)
  • Avoid excessive irrigation
  • Allow tubers to mature before harvest
Potato Common Scab

Streptomyces scabies

Description

Common scab is a soil-borne bacterial disease that affects the quality of potato tubers. It is most severe in dry, alkaline soils with pH above 5.5.

Symptoms
  • Rough, corky, raised lesions on tuber surfaces
  • Lesions may be superficial or deep
  • Lesions range from brown to tan to reddish
  • No visible symptoms on above-ground parts
  • Affects marketability but not yield
Treatment
  • No effective treatment once tubers are infected
  • Maintain soil moisture during tuber formation
  • Lower soil pH with sulfur amendments
  • Affected tubers are still edible after peeling
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Use certified disease-free seed potatoes
  • Maintain soil pH between 5.0 and 5.2
  • Practice crop rotation (3-4 years)
  • Avoid using fresh manure before planting
Potato Ring Rot

Clavibacter michiganensis

Description

Ring rot is a serious bacterial disease of potatoes that is subject to strict quarantine regulations in many countries. It can survive for years on equipment and storage facilities.

Symptoms
  • Wilting and yellowing of leaves, often on one side
  • Stems may show creamy-yellow discoloration when cut
  • Characteristic yellow to brown ring of rot in tuber vascular tissue
  • Advanced infections cause tuber breakdown and hollow centers
  • Some infections may be latent with no visible symptoms
Treatment
  • No chemical treatments available
  • Remove and destroy all infected plants and tubers
  • Disinfect all equipment and storage facilities
  • Report suspected cases to agricultural authorities
Prevention
  • Use certified disease-free seed potatoes
  • Practice strict sanitation of all equipment
  • Clean and disinfect storage areas
  • Avoid cutting seed potatoes
  • Practice crop rotation
Rice Blast

Magnaporthe oryzae

Description

Rice blast is one of the most destructive diseases of rice worldwide. It can affect all above-ground parts of the rice plant and occurs in both tropical and temperate environments.

Symptoms
  • Diamond-shaped lesions with gray centers on leaves
  • Brown to black spots on stems and leaf collars
  • Infected panicles may break or appear white and empty
  • Reduced grain quality and yield
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing azoxystrobin or trifloxystrobin
  • Maintain proper water management
  • Balance nitrogen fertilization
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Use certified disease-free seeds
  • Avoid excessive nitrogen application
  • Maintain proper spacing between plants
  • Apply silicon-based fertilizers
Rice Bacterial Leaf Blight

Xanthomonas oryzae pv. oryzae

Description

Bacterial leaf blight is a serious disease of rice in irrigated and rainfed environments. It is particularly destructive in tropical and subtropical Asia.

Symptoms
  • Water-soaked lesions at leaf margins that turn yellow to white
  • Lesions expand along veins and may cover entire leaves
  • Milky bacterial ooze visible on young lesions
  • Infected leaves dry up and die
  • Severe infections cause wilting of seedlings (kresek)
Treatment
  • Apply copper-based bactericides
  • Drain fields to reduce humidity
  • Avoid excessive nitrogen application
  • Remove and destroy infected plants
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Use disease-free seeds
  • Practice crop rotation
  • Maintain proper spacing between plants
  • Avoid clipping leaf tips during transplanting
Rice Sheath Blight

Rhizoctonia solani

Description

Sheath blight is a major fungal disease of rice that occurs in all rice-growing regions. It is particularly severe in intensive production systems with high nitrogen use.

Symptoms
  • Initial water-soaked, greenish-gray lesions on leaf sheaths near water line
  • Lesions enlarge with irregular margins and grayish-white centers
  • Infection spreads upward to leaves and panicles
  • Sclerotia (survival structures) form on infected tissues
  • Severe infections cause lodging and unfilled grains
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing azoxystrobin or propiconazole
  • Reduce nitrogen application
  • Drain fields temporarily
  • Remove and destroy infected plant debris
Prevention
  • Plant moderately resistant varieties
  • Use balanced fertilization
  • Maintain proper spacing between plants
  • Practice field sanitation
  • Rotate with non-host crops
Rice Brown Spot

Cochliobolus miyabeanus

Description

Brown spot is a common fungal disease of rice that is often associated with nutrient-deficient soils. It contributed to the Bengal famine of 1943 and remains a significant threat in low-input rice production.

Symptoms
  • Small, circular to oval brown lesions on leaves
  • Lesions have dark brown margins and gray to white centers
  • Spots may coalesce in severe infections
  • Brown to black spots on glumes and grains
  • Infected seeds may break during milling
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing propiconazole or iprodione
  • Correct soil nutrient deficiencies, especially potassium
  • Apply balanced fertilization
  • Remove and destroy infected plant debris
Prevention
  • Use disease-free seeds
  • Treat seeds with hot water or fungicides
  • Maintain proper soil fertility
  • Practice field sanitation
  • Avoid water stress during grain filling
Rice Tungro Disease

Rice tungro bacilliform virus and Rice tungro spherical virus

Description

Tungro is a viral disease complex that is one of the most damaging diseases of rice in South and Southeast Asia. It is transmitted by green leafhoppers and can cause significant yield losses.

Symptoms
  • Stunted plants with reduced tillering
  • Yellow to orange discoloration of leaves
  • Mottled or striped leaf appearance
  • Delayed flowering and reduced panicle size
  • Incomplete grain filling and reduced yield
Treatment
  • No direct treatment for infected plants
  • Control leafhopper vectors with insecticides
  • Remove and destroy infected plants
  • Avoid staggered planting in the same area
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Synchronize planting in an area
  • Maintain field sanitation
  • Control weed hosts of leafhoppers
  • Use proper spacing to reduce leafhopper movement
Wheat Stem Rust

Puccinia graminis

Description

Wheat stem rust is a devastating fungal disease that affects wheat, barley, and other cereal crops. It has caused major epidemics throughout history and continues to threaten global wheat production.

Symptoms
  • Reddish-brown, elongated pustules on stems and leaves
  • Pustules have ragged edges and can be rubbed off
  • Severe infections cause stems to weaken and lodge
  • Reduced grain filling and shriveled kernels
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing tebuconazole or propiconazole
  • Early detection and treatment is crucial
  • Remove alternate hosts like barberry plants
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Use certified disease-free seeds
  • Plant early-maturing varieties
  • Apply preventive fungicides
  • Monitor fields regularly
Wheat Leaf Rust

Puccinia triticina

Description

Leaf rust is one of the most common diseases of wheat worldwide. It is less destructive than stem rust but more widespread and occurs annually in many wheat-growing regions.

Symptoms
  • Small, round to oval, orange-brown pustules on leaf surfaces
  • Pustules are randomly scattered on leaves
  • Severe infections cause leaf yellowing and premature leaf death
  • Reduction in photosynthetic area leads to smaller grain size
  • Can be distinguished from stem rust by smaller, rounder pustules
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing triazole or strobilurin
  • Timing of application is critical - apply at first sign of disease
  • Multiple applications may be necessary in severe cases
  • Maintain proper crop nutrition to improve plant resilience
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Rotate crops to reduce inoculum
  • Eliminate volunteer wheat plants
  • Plant early in the season when possible
  • Apply preventive fungicides at critical growth stages
Wheat Powdery Mildew

Blumeria graminis f. sp. tritici

Description

Powdery mildew is a common fungal disease of wheat that thrives in humid conditions with moderate temperatures. It is most severe in dense canopies and high nitrogen fertility situations.

Symptoms
  • White, powdery patches on leaves, stems, and heads
  • Patches turn grayish-brown as they age
  • Infected leaves may turn yellow and die prematurely
  • Lower leaves are typically infected first
  • Black fruiting bodies (cleistothecia) may develop in older colonies
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing triazole or strobilurin
  • Sulfur-based fungicides can be effective
  • Reduce nitrogen application if disease is present
  • Improve air circulation by reducing plant density
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Use balanced fertilization (avoid excess nitrogen)
  • Maintain proper plant spacing
  • Rotate with non-host crops
  • Apply preventive fungicides at early growth stages
Wheat Fusarium Head Blight

Fusarium graminearum

Description

Fusarium head blight (FHB), also known as scab, is a devastating disease that affects wheat and other small grains. It not only reduces yield but also contaminates grain with mycotoxins that are harmful to humans and animals.

Symptoms
  • Bleached spikelets on wheat heads
  • Pink or orange spore masses on infected heads in humid conditions
  • Shriveled, chalky white or pink kernels (tombstones)
  • Premature death of infected spikelets
  • Mycotoxin contamination (deoxynivalenol/DON) in harvested grain
Treatment
  • Apply triazole fungicides at flowering stage
  • Adjust combine settings to remove infected kernels during harvest
  • Test harvested grain for mycotoxin levels
  • Segregate heavily infected grain
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Rotate with non-host crops (not corn)
  • Manage residue through tillage if appropriate
  • Apply preventive fungicides at flowering
  • Stagger planting dates to avoid uniform flowering during wet periods
Wheat Take-all

Gaeumannomyces graminis var. tritici

Description

Take-all is a serious root disease of wheat and other cereals. It is caused by a soil-borne fungus that infects the roots and crown, disrupting water and nutrient uptake.

Symptoms
  • Stunted plants occurring in patches
  • Whiteheads (prematurely ripened heads with no grain)
  • Black, shiny lesions on roots and crown
  • Brittle roots that break easily when pulled
  • Plants easily pulled from soil due to poor root development
Treatment
  • No effective chemical treatments once infected
  • Improve drainage in affected fields
  • Maintain adequate soil fertility, especially phosphorus
  • Apply manganese in deficient soils
Prevention
  • Rotate with non-host crops (not grasses)
  • Use long rotations (2-3 years) away from wheat
  • Control grass weeds that can host the pathogen
  • Maintain proper soil pH (below 7.0 if possible)
  • Consider seed treatments with silthiofam or pseudomonas bacteria
Grape Downy Mildew

Plasmopara viticola

Description

Downy mildew is a serious disease of grapes that can cause significant crop losses. It thrives in humid, wet conditions and can spread rapidly through vineyards.

Symptoms
  • Yellow, oily spots on upper leaf surfaces
  • White, downy growth on leaf undersides
  • Brown, irregular lesions as spots mature
  • Young berries become covered with white mold
  • Infected berries turn brown, shrivel, and fall off
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing copper, mancozeb, or phosphorous acid
  • Remove and destroy infected leaves and fruit
  • Improve air circulation by leaf removal around fruit zones
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Orient rows to maximize sun exposure and air movement
  • Prune to improve canopy airflow
  • Apply preventive fungicides before rainfall
  • Use weather forecasting to time fungicide applications
Grape Powdery Mildew

Erysiphe necator

Description

Powdery mildew is one of the most widespread and destructive diseases of grapevines. Unlike downy mildew, it can develop in both dry and humid conditions and doesn't require free water for infection.

Symptoms
  • White, powdery fungal growth on leaves, shoots, and fruit
  • Distorted, stunted growth of new shoots
  • Reduced fruit quality and yield
  • Infected berries may crack, exposing seeds
  • Fruit develops off-flavors in wine production
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing sulfur, sterol inhibitors, or strobilurins
  • Remove and destroy infected plant parts
  • Apply potassium bicarbonate or horticultural oils for organic control
  • Improve canopy management to increase air circulation
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Prune to improve air circulation and sun exposure
  • Apply preventive fungicides at bud break
  • Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilization
  • Orient rows to maximize airflow
Grape Black Rot

Guignardia bidwellii

Description

Black rot is a serious fungal disease of grapes that can cause complete crop loss in warm, humid climates. It affects all green parts of the vine but is most damaging to the berries.

Symptoms
  • Small, circular, reddish-brown spots on leaves
  • Tan centers in leaf spots with dark borders
  • Black, sunken lesions on stems and fruit stalks
  • Infected berries develop brown spots that spread rapidly
  • Berries shrivel into hard, black mummies that remain attached to clusters
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing myclobutanil, tebuconazole, or strobilurins
  • Remove mummified berries from vines
  • Maintain fungicide protection during critical period (bloom to 4-5 weeks after)
  • Remove infected leaves and shoots
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Prune and destroy all mummified fruit
  • Cultivate soil beneath vines to bury mummies
  • Improve air circulation through proper pruning
  • Apply preventive fungicides before rainfall events
Grape Anthracnose

Elsinoe ampelina

Description

Anthracnose, also known as bird's-eye rot, is a fungal disease that affects all green parts of the grapevine. It is particularly severe in warm, humid regions with frequent rainfall.

Symptoms
  • Circular, sunken lesions with gray centers and dark margins on leaves
  • Elongated, sunken lesions on shoots, petioles, and tendrils
  • Characteristic "bird's-eye" spots on berries (circular with gray centers and reddish-brown margins)
  • Cracking of infected berries
  • Withering and dying of severely infected shoots
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing mancozeb, captan, or strobilurins
  • Remove and destroy infected plant parts
  • Apply lime sulfur during dormant season
  • Maintain fungicide protection during wet periods
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Prune and destroy infected wood during dormancy
  • Apply dormant sprays of lime sulfur
  • Ensure good air circulation through proper canopy management
  • Avoid overhead irrigation
Grape Crown Gall

Agrobacterium vitis

Description

Crown gall is a bacterial disease that affects many woody plants, including grapevines. It causes tumorous growths that disrupt the vascular system, weakening and sometimes killing the vine.

Symptoms
  • Rough, irregular galls or tumors on trunks, cordons, and canes
  • Galls initially appear white or flesh-colored, later turning brown and corky
  • Stunted growth and reduced vigor
  • Yellowing and premature defoliation
  • Increased susceptibility to winter injury
Treatment
  • No effective chemical treatments once infected
  • Remove small galls on larger vines by cutting into healthy wood
  • Remove and destroy severely infected vines
  • Protect pruning wounds with wound sealants
Prevention
  • Plant certified disease-free vines
  • Use resistant rootstocks
  • Avoid planting in sites with a history of crown gall
  • Minimize winter injury by proper site selection
  • Disinfect pruning tools between vines
Soybean Rust

Phakopsora pachyrhizi

Description

Soybean rust is an aggressive fungal disease that can cause significant yield losses. It was first detected in the United States in 2004 and has since become a concern for soybean producers.

Symptoms
  • Small, yellow or brown spots on leaves
  • Raised pustules within lesions
  • Rust-colored spores visible within pustules
  • Premature defoliation
  • Reduced pod fill and seed quality
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing triazoles or strobilurins
  • Timing of application is critical - apply at first detection
  • Multiple applications may be necessary
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Plant early-maturing varieties
  • Monitor fields regularly
  • Use preventive fungicides in high-risk areas
  • Follow regional rust forecasts and alerts
Sudden Death Syndrome

Fusarium virguliforme

Description

Sudden Death Syndrome (SDS) is a destructive soil-borne fungal disease that affects soybeans. It typically appears during reproductive stages and can cause significant yield losses, especially when combined with soybean cyst nematode infections.

Symptoms
  • Interveinal chlorosis and necrosis on leaves (yellow and brown between veins)
  • Leaf tissue death while leaf veins remain green
  • Premature defoliation with petioles remaining attached
  • Blue-gray spore masses on roots
  • Root rot and discoloration of vascular tissue in stems
Treatment
  • No effective in-season treatments available
  • Manage drainage in affected fields
  • Reduce soil compaction
  • Manage soybean cyst nematode if present
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Delay planting until soil warms
  • Improve field drainage
  • Rotate with non-host crops
  • Consider seed treatments with fluopyram
Phytophthora Root Rot

Phytophthora sojae

Description

Phytophthora root rot is a destructive disease of soybeans caused by a soil-borne water mold. It is most severe in poorly drained, compacted soils and can affect soybeans at any growth stage.

Symptoms
  • Seed rot and pre-emergence damping off
  • Yellowing and wilting of leaves
  • Brown discoloration of stem extending upward from soil line
  • Rotted, discolored roots with outer tissue that sloughs off easily
  • Plants easily pulled from soil due to root damage
Treatment
  • No effective treatments once plants are infected
  • Improve drainage in affected areas
  • Reduce soil compaction
  • Metalaxyl or mefenoxam seed treatments can protect seedlings
Prevention
  • Plant varieties with Rps resistance genes
  • Use seed treatments containing metalaxyl or mefenoxam
  • Improve field drainage
  • Rotate with non-host crops
  • Avoid planting in cool, wet conditions
Frogeye Leaf Spot

Cercospora sojina

Description

Frogeye leaf spot is a common foliar disease of soybeans caused by a fungal pathogen. It can cause significant yield losses, particularly in warm, humid environments and on susceptible varieties.

Symptoms
  • Circular to angular spots with gray centers and reddish-purple margins
  • Lesions typically 1-5 mm in diameter
  • Centers of older lesions may fall out, creating a shot-hole appearance
  • Lesions can also appear on stems, pods, and seeds
  • Severe infections cause premature defoliation
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing strobilurins, triazoles, or mixed modes of action
  • Apply at R1-R3 growth stages for best results
  • Rotate fungicide modes of action to prevent resistance
  • Monitor fields after application for disease progression
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Rotate with non-host crops
  • Plow under crop residue
  • Use pathogen-free seed
  • Apply preventive fungicides in high-risk situations
Soybean Cyst Nematode

Heterodera glycines

Description

Soybean cyst nematode (SCN) is the most damaging pathogen of soybeans worldwide. It is a microscopic roundworm that infects soybean roots, often causing significant yield losses without obvious above-ground symptoms.

Symptoms
  • Stunted plants occurring in oval-shaped patches in fields
  • Yellowing of leaves (chlorosis)
  • Reduced root mass and nodulation
  • Small, white to yellow lemon-shaped cysts visible on roots
  • Often no visible symptoms despite yield loss ("silent yield robber")
Treatment
  • No effective in-season treatments available
  • Nematicide seed treatments provide early-season protection
  • Maintain optimal soil fertility and pH
  • Avoid moving soil from infested fields
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties (rotate resistance sources)
  • Rotate with non-host crops (corn, small grains)
  • Use nematicide seed treatments
  • Test soil regularly to monitor SCN population levels
  • Clean equipment between fields to prevent spread
Banana Black Sigatoka

Mycosphaerella fijiensis

Description

Black Sigatoka is the most destructive leaf disease of bananas worldwide. It reduces photosynthetic area, leading to significant yield losses and premature ripening of fruit.

Symptoms
  • Small, yellow spots on leaves that enlarge and turn brown
  • Brown spots with gray centers and yellow halos
  • Spots merge to form large necrotic areas
  • Premature leaf death and defoliation
  • Reduced fruit size and premature ripening
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides on a regular schedule
  • Rotate fungicide groups to prevent resistance
  • Remove infected leaves
  • Improve drainage in plantation
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Use disease-free planting material
  • Maintain proper plant spacing
  • Implement proper drainage
  • Regular deleafing of infected material
Panama Disease

Fusarium oxysporum f. sp. cubense

Description

Panama disease, also known as Fusarium wilt, is a devastating soil-borne fungal disease that attacks the roots and vascular system of banana plants. Tropical Race 4 (TR4) is particularly destructive to Cavendish bananas.

Symptoms
  • Yellowing of older leaves, progressing from margin to midrib
  • Leaves collapse at the petiole and hang down the pseudostem
  • Reddish-brown discoloration of vascular tissue when stem is cut
  • Splitting of the pseudostem near the soil line
  • Plants eventually wilt and die
Treatment
  • No effective chemical treatments available
  • Remove and destroy infected plants
  • Quarantine affected areas
  • Disinfect tools and equipment
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties
  • Use disease-free planting material
  • Implement strict biosecurity measures
  • Avoid moving soil or plants from infected areas
  • Maintain good drainage and soil health
Bunchy Top Disease

Banana bunchy top virus (BBTV)

Description

Banana bunchy top disease (BBTD) is caused by a virus and is one of the most serious diseases of bananas. It is transmitted by the banana aphid and through infected planting material.

Symptoms
  • Dark green streaks on leaves and leaf stems
  • Narrow, upright leaves with yellow margins
  • Leaves become progressively smaller and bunched at the top
  • Plants have a rosette or bunchy appearance
  • Infected plants rarely produce fruit
Treatment
  • No cure for infected plants
  • Remove and destroy infected plants immediately
  • Control aphid vectors with insecticides
  • Establish aphid-free buffer zones
Prevention
  • Use virus-indexed tissue culture plants
  • Inspect plants regularly for early symptoms
  • Control aphid populations
  • Maintain quarantine measures
  • Avoid moving planting material from affected areas
Banana Bacterial Wilt

Xanthomonas campestris pv. musacearum

Description

Banana bacterial wilt (BBW), also known as banana Xanthomonas wilt (BXW), is a devastating bacterial disease affecting bananas and plantains in East and Central Africa. It can cause up to 100% yield loss in severely affected areas.

Symptoms
  • Yellowing and wilting of leaves
  • Yellow ooze when stem is cut
  • Premature ripening and internal discoloration of fruit
  • Withering and rotting of male buds
  • Death of the entire plant
Treatment
  • No chemical treatments available
  • Remove and destroy infected plants
  • Disinfect tools with bleach or fire
  • Remove male buds with a forked stick
Prevention
  • Use clean planting materials
  • Disinfect farm tools between plants
  • Remove male buds early with a forked stick
  • Avoid moving plant material from infected areas
  • Practice crop rotation where possible
Banana Anthracnose

Colletotrichum musae

Description

Anthracnose is a common post-harvest disease of bananas that affects the fruit during ripening. It can cause significant losses during storage, transportation, and marketing.

Symptoms
  • Small, dark spots on the fruit skin
  • Spots enlarge and become sunken as fruit ripens
  • Lesions develop salmon-colored spore masses in humid conditions
  • Affected areas may become soft and rot
  • Black lesions with yellow halos in severe cases
Treatment
  • Post-harvest hot water treatment
  • Apply fungicides before harvest
  • Maintain proper storage temperature
  • Handle fruit carefully to avoid wounds
Prevention
  • Maintain field sanitation
  • Protect fruit from damage during harvest
  • Control insects that may cause wounds
  • Apply pre-harvest fungicides
  • Maintain proper humidity and temperature during storage
Tomato Early Blight

Alternaria solani

Description

Early blight is a common fungal disease of tomatoes that can affect all above-ground parts of the plant. It typically appears on older leaves first and progresses upward.

Symptoms
  • Dark brown spots with concentric rings (target-like pattern)
  • Yellow areas surrounding the spots
  • Spots may merge, causing leaves to wither and die
  • Stem lesions are dark, slightly sunken
  • Fruit lesions are dark, leathery, and often at stem end
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing chlorothalonil or copper
  • Remove and destroy infected leaves
  • Improve air circulation by pruning
  • Avoid overhead watering
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties

Tomato Septoria Leaf Spot

Septoria lycopersici

Description

Septoria leaf spot is a common fungal disease that affects tomatoes, particularly during warm, wet seasons. It primarily affects the foliage and can cause significant defoliation.

Symptoms
  • Small, circular spots with dark borders and light centers
  • Spots typically appear on lower leaves first
  • Tiny black dots (pycnidia) may appear in the center of spots
  • Leaves may turn yellow, then brown, and fall off
  • Rarely affects fruit directly
Treatment
  • Apply fungicides containing chlorothalonil or copper
  • Remove and destroy infected leaves
  • Maintain good air circulation
  • Apply treatments every 7-10 days during wet weather
Prevention
  • Practice crop rotation (2-3 years)
  • Remove plant debris at the end of the season
  • Avoid wetting foliage when watering
  • Space plants properly for good air circulation
  • Mulch around plants to prevent spore splash
Tomato Bacterial Spot

Xanthomonas spp.

Description

Bacterial spot is a serious disease affecting tomatoes and peppers. It can occur at any growth stage and is particularly severe in warm, wet weather.

Symptoms
  • Small, water-soaked spots on leaves, stems, and fruit
  • Spots enlarge and turn dark brown to black
  • Leaf spots may have yellow halos
  • Spots on fruit start as small black specks and develop into raised scabs
  • Severe infections cause leaf yellowing and defoliation
Treatment
  • Apply copper-based bactericides
  • Remove and destroy infected plants
  • Avoid handling plants when wet
  • Rotate with non-host crops
Prevention
  • Use disease-free seeds and transplants
  • Practice crop rotation (2-3 years)
  • Avoid overhead irrigation
  • Sanitize garden tools
  • Apply preventive copper sprays during humid weather
Tomato Fusarium Wilt

Fusarium oxysporum

Description

Fusarium wilt is a soil-borne fungal disease that affects tomatoes worldwide. It enters through the roots and blocks the water-conducting vessels of the plant.

Symptoms
  • Yellowing of lower leaves, often on one side of the plant
  • Wilting during hot days with recovery at night
  • Progressive wilting and death of foliage
  • Brown discoloration of vascular tissue when stem is cut
  • Stunted growth and reduced yield
Treatment
  • No effective chemical treatment once infected
  • Remove and destroy infected plants
  • Solarize soil in hot climates
  • Maintain optimal growing conditions to reduce stress
Prevention
  • Plant resistant varieties (labeled F or FF)
  • Practice long crop rotations (5-7 years)
  • Use disease-free transplants
  • Maintain soil pH around 6.5-7.0
  • Use raised beds for better drainage
Tomato Blossom End Rot

Physiological disorder

Description

Blossom end rot is a physiological disorder caused by calcium deficiency in the developing fruit. It is not a disease but rather a condition related to environmental factors and plant nutrition.

Symptoms
  • Water-soaked spots at the blossom end (bottom) of fruit
  • Spots enlarge and become dark, leathery, and sunken
  • Secondary pathogens may invade the damaged tissue
  • Most common on first fruits of the season
  • More prevalent during drought conditions or inconsistent watering
Treatment
  • Maintain consistent soil moisture
  • Apply calcium sprays to developing fruit
  • Remove affected fruit
  • Add calcium supplements to soil
Prevention
  • Test soil and adjust pH to 6.5-6.8
  • Add calcium to soil before planting
  • Use mulch to maintain even soil moisture
  • Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilization
  • Water deeply and consistently
